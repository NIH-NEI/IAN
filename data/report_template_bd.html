RELB-mediated Immune Dysregulation in Behcet’s Disease

1. Integrated Analysis Results

This report summarizes the results of a multi-agent-AI-system (IAN) based analysis performed using the provided list of differentially expressed genes. Results after IAN integrated all the individual agents responses is provided below:

1.1. High-Level Summary

Behcet’s disease pathogenesis involves a complex interplay between peripheral and central inflammation. Peripheral immune dysregulation, characterized by complement activation (C1QA, C1QB, C1QC, C2, CFB), cytokine signaling, and chemokine signaling (CXCL1, CXCL5, CX3CR1), is a primary driver. Macrophage activation (CD68) plays a crucial role. These inflammatory processes may trigger neuroinflammation (Agent 1) and are potentially linked to infectious triggers (Agent 2). Dysregulation of GPCR signaling (Agent 3) and ECM remodeling further contribute. The transcription factor RELB appears to be a central orchestrator of these processes, regulating the expression of numerous chemokines and their receptors. Albumin (ALB) may serve as a systemic indicator of the inflammatory state.

1.2. Affected Biological Processes

Integrating the agent summaries and the provided data reveals a consistent picture of dysregulated immune responses in BD. The core processes implicated across all analyses are:

  • Complement activation: Multiple pathways (KEGG, WikiPathways, Reactome) and GO terms consistently highlight the involvement of complement components (C1QA, C1QB, C1QC, C2, CFB).

  • Cytokine and chemokine signaling: KEGG, WikiPathways, Reactome, and GO analyses all show enrichment of pathways and terms related to cytokine-cytokine receptor interactions and chemokine-mediated signaling. Key genes include CXCL1, CXCL5, CXCR1, CXCR2, and CX3CR1.

  • Macrophage activation: CD68, a macrophage marker, consistently appears in multiple analyses with high STRING network scores, indicating a central role for macrophages in the inflammatory response.

  • ECM remodeling: Several pathways and GO terms point to alterations in ECM components (COL1A1, VTN, ACAN, COMP).

  • Neuronal function (Agent 1): Agent 1 uniquely highlights neuronal pathways, suggesting neuroinflammation.

  • Infectious disease pathways (Agent 2): Agent 2 uniquely highlights pathways associated with bacterial and other infections, suggesting a potential link between infection and BD pathogenesis.

  • GPCR signaling (Agent 3): Agent 3 uniquely emphasizes the involvement of various GPCR signaling pathways, potentially mediating inflammatory responses and immune cell migration.

The overlap genes (present in multiple pathway analyses) reinforce the importance of these core processes. Unique genes in each analysis suggest additional layers of complexity and specialized roles within the overall inflammatory response.

1.3. Potential Regulatory Networks

The ChEA analysis strongly implicates RELB as a master regulator. Its target genes (CCDC71L, PPP1R15A, ANKRD37, SPHK1, ROM1, TNFAIP3, CXCL1, AP5B1, LTC4S, KAZALD1, CXCL5, SOCS3, DDIT4, CDC42EP2, HBEGF) are involved in multiple enriched pathways, primarily related to immune responses. The interactions between RELB and its target genes, many of which are chemokines or chemokine receptors, are largely supported by the STRING data. The novel interactions identified by ChEA (e.g., RELB-ANKRD37, RELB-CCDC71L) require further investigation to determine their functional significance in BD. Besides RELB, other potential upstream regulators include:

  • NF-κB: Consistently implicated across multiple analyses due to the enrichment of TNF receptor signaling pathways.

  • AP-1 (Jun/Fos): The presence of FOSL1 and JUND, and their interaction (high STRING score), suggests AP-1’s involvement in regulating genes in inflammatory pathways.

  • STAT family members: The strong enrichment of cytokine pathways suggests the involvement of STAT transcription factors.

  • CEBPB: This transcription factor is consistently implicated in multiple analyses and has high STRING network scores, suggesting a role in regulating immune responses.

  • PPARs: Agent 2 suggests a potential role for PPARs in lipid metabolism and inflammation.

1.4. Potential Hub Genes

Based on the integrated analysis and STRING network properties, the following genes emerge as potential hub genes:

  • ALB: Highest combined network score in STRING, involved in multiple processes (immune response, lipid metabolism, ECM).

  • CX3CR1: Enriched in multiple pathways (immune response, neuronal function, GPCR signaling) and GO terms.

  • C1QA, C1QB, C1QC: Core complement components, enriched in multiple pathways and GO terms.

  • RELB: Identified as a potential upstream regulator by ChEA, regulating genes involved in inflammation and immune response.

  • CD68: High STRING score, central to macrophage activation.

  • CXCL1: High STRING score, key player in chemokine signaling.

  • APOE: High STRING score, involved in lipid metabolism and inflammation.

These genes are consistently enriched across multiple pathways and GO terms and often exhibit high STRING network scores. Their central positions in the STRING network further support their hub gene status.

1.5. Similar Systems

The observed pathways and interactions are similar to those reported in other inflammatory and autoimmune diseases, including systemic lupus erythematosus, rheumatoid arthritis, multiple sclerosis, and inflammatory bowel disease.

1.6. Novelty Exploration

The strong enrichment of neuronal pathways (Agent 1) in PBMCs is potentially novel, suggesting a peripheral-central inflammation link. The unexpected connections between complement activation and neuronal pathways warrant further investigation. The involvement of infectious disease pathways (Agent 2) may also represent a novel aspect of BD pathogenesis. The extensive GPCR signaling involvement (Agent 3) also requires further exploration.

2. System Model

IAN takes the integrated full analysis report and the integrated system network data from each of the agents response and puts forward a system model, which includes the system overview, mechanistic model and an hypothesis.

2.1. System Model and Overview

The provided network depicts a complex interplay of genes and pathways primarily involved in immune response, inflammation, complement activation, and extracellular matrix (ECM) interactions. The differentially expressed genes (DEGs) identified from Behcet’s disease (BD) patients, compared to healthy controls (HC), provide crucial context for understanding the system’s dysregulation in this autoimmune disease. The network highlights the central roles of several key genes, including RELB, ALB, APOE, C1QA, C1QB, C1QC, CX3CR1, CD68, TYROBP, and SPI1, in orchestrating these processes. The interactions suggest a complex interplay between innate and adaptive immunity, with potential implications for vascular function and tissue remodeling.

2.2. Mechanistic Model

The identified relationships suggest a mechanistic model where the upstream regulators and key genes influence the BD phenotype through their downstream targets and pathways. Dysregulation of RELB leads to altered expression of its target genes (CXCL1, CXCL5, TNFAIP3), resulting in an amplified inflammatory response. ALB’s involvement in multiple pathways suggests a contribution to vascular dysfunction and tissue damage. The complement components (C1QA, C1QB, C1QC) contribute to the inflammatory cascade through activation of the complement system. APOE’s involvement links lipid metabolism to inflammation and vascular dysfunction. CX3CR1, CD68, TYROBP, and SPI1 contribute to immune cell recruitment, activation, and tissue remodeling. The interactions between these genes suggest a complex interplay between innate and adaptive immunity, leading to the characteristic inflammation and vascular complications of BD.

2.3. Hypothesis

Behcet’s disease pathogenesis involves a systemic dysregulation of immune responses, complement activation, and ECM interactions. We hypothesize that dysregulation of RELB, ALB, APOE, and the complement system (C1QA, C1QB, C1QC) leads to altered expression of their target genes and pathways, resulting in an amplified inflammatory response, impaired immune regulation, and altered tissue remodeling, contributing to the development and progression of BD. The specific interactions between these key players, particularly the interplay between inflammatory chemokines (CXCL1, CXCL5) and immune cell activation markers (CD68, TYROBP, SPI1), are crucial in driving the disease phenotype. Further investigation into the specific molecular mechanisms underlying the dysregulation of these genes and pathways is needed to fully elucidate the pathogenesis of BD.

Agent 6: 1. Summary of Interactions:

The STRING analysis reveals a highly interconnected network of proteins, with several exhibiting high combined network scores. Albumin (ALB), a major plasma protein, shows strong interactions with multiple proteins involved in immune response (e.g., CD68, FCGR1A, C1QA), lipid metabolism (APOE, LPL), and extracellular matrix (ECM) components (ACAN, COL1A1). Other highly connected proteins include PRKACB (protein kinase A catalytic subunit beta), a key enzyme in various signaling pathways, and CD68 (a macrophage marker) with connections to immune and complement system components. These interactions suggest a complex interplay between inflammation, lipid metabolism, and ECM remodeling in Behcet’s disease.

2. Key Interactions and Network Topology:

Based on the combined network scores, ALB, PRKACB, CD68, CXCL1, APOE, CX3CR1, and PXDNL stand out as key proteins. ALB’s extensive interactions highlight its central role in the network, potentially reflecting its involvement in multiple disease processes. PRKACB’s numerous interactions suggest its importance in various signaling pathways. CD68, a marker of macrophages, is highly connected to immune and complement components, indicating macrophage activation. CXCL1 (chemokine ligand 5), CX3CR1 (chemokine receptor 1), and APOE (apolipoprotein E) are involved in immune cell recruitment and lipid metabolism. PXDNL (paxillin) is involved in cell adhesion and cytoskeletal organization.

The network structure appears modular, with several clusters of highly interconnected proteins. The complement system proteins (C1QA, C1QB, C1QC) form a tightly connected cluster, as do several chemokines and their receptors (CXCL1, CXCL5, CXCL16, CX3CR1, CXCR1, CXCR2, CCR8, CCR9, XCR1, CCL4L1, CCL4L2, CCL14). ALB acts as a central hub, connecting these modules. This modularity suggests that distinct biological processes are coordinated to contribute to the overall disease phenotype.

4. Cross-Analysis:

Several connections exist between the high-scoring proteins and the enriched pathways. ALB is present in pathways related to complement and coagulation cascades, lipid metabolism, and ECM-receptor interaction. PRKACB is involved in multiple pathways, including chemokine signaling, circadian entrainment, taste transduction, and various GPCR signaling pathways. CD68 is involved in efferocytosis and immune response pathways. CXCL1, CXCL5, CXCL16, CX3CR1, CXCR1, CXCR2, CCR8, CCR9, XCR1, CCL4L1, CCL4L2, and CCL14 are all involved in cytokine-cytokine receptor interaction and chemokine signaling pathways.

RELB, identified in the ChEA analysis, regulates several genes also present in the interaction network (e.g., TNFAIP3, CXCL1, CXCL5, SOCS3). This suggests that RELB could be a key upstream regulator of the observed interactions.
