1. Integrated Analysis Results

1.1. High-Level Summary

Uveitis pathogenesis involves a complex interplay between dysregulated immune responses (driven by complement activation and T cell co-stimulation), impaired ECM remodeling (mediated by FN1 and other ECM proteins), and potentially infectious triggers. Upstream regulators like NF-κB, IRFs, AP-1, SMAD family members, TFAP2C, and POU5F1 likely orchestrate these processes. The involvement of FN1 in ECM remodeling and its high network connectivity suggests a central role in coordinating these processes. The strong complement activation indicates a significant contribution of innate immunity. The involvement of CD274 highlights the importance of immune checkpoint regulation. The presence of Y-chromosome genes suggests a potential sex-specific component.

1.2. Affected Biological Processes

The integrated analysis reveals a highly interconnected network involving immune dysregulation, complement activation, ECM remodeling, and potentially neuronal signaling (Agent 3). The overlap in pathway results across agents strongly supports this conclusion.

  • Overlap Genes: Genes like FN1, C1QA, C1QB, C1QC, CD274, and ANK2 appear consistently across multiple pathway analyses (KEGG, WikiPathways, Reactome, GO), highlighting their central roles. These genes are involved in ECM organization (FN1, COL4A3, COL4A4), complement activation (C1QA, C1QB, C1QC, SERPING1), and immune regulation (CD274, GBP5, GBP1).

  • Unique Genes: Unique genes in each pathway analysis suggest specialized roles within the broader context. For example, Y-chromosome genes (UTY, KDM5D, USP9Y, DDX3Y, RPS4Y1, EIF1AY) are enriched, potentially indicating sex-specific aspects of uveitis. Neuronal-related genes (Agent 3) might suggest a less direct, but still relevant, connection between the immune response and neurological processes.

1.3. Potential Regulatory Networks

The ChEA analysis (Agent 4) reveals significant enrichment of transcription factors RELB, SMAD4, TFAP2C, and POU5F1. These TFs provide a regulatory framework for the observed gene expression changes.

  • RELB (NF-κB family): Regulates genes involved in immune responses (GBP5, CD274), aligning with the strong complement and interferon signaling pathways.

  • SMAD4 (TGF-β signaling): Regulates genes involved in ECM remodeling (FN1, COL4A3, COL4A4) and cell adhesion, consistent with the observed pathway enrichments.

  • TFAP2C: Involved in cell differentiation and development, potentially influencing the cellular context of the immune response.

  • POU5F1: Associated with pluripotency, its role in uveitis requires further investigation.

2. System Model

IAN takes the integrated full analysis report and the integrated system network data from each of the agents response and puts forward a system model, which includes the system overview, mechanistic model and an hypothesis.

2.1. System Model and Overview

The provided network, informed by RNA-Seq data from uveitis patients and healthy controls, centers on a complex interplay of genes and pathways primarily involved in immune response, complement activation, cell adhesion, and extracellular matrix (ECM) remodeling. The differentially expressed genes (DEGs) highlight the importance of these processes in the pathogenesis of uveitis. The network reveals interconnected modules, with key hubs like FN1 (fibronectin), C1QA/C1QB (complement components), CD274 (PD-L1), and several transcription factors (e.g., RELB, SMAD4, ATF3) playing central roles. The diverse etiologies of uveitis within the cohort (axial spondyloarthritis, sarcoidosis, etc.) likely contribute to variations in the specific DEG profiles, although the core pathways remain consistent.

2.2. Mechanistic Model

The identified relationships suggest a mechanistic model where upstream regulators like RELB and SMAD4, along with ATF3, influence the uveitis phenotype through their downstream targets. RELB’s regulation of GBP5 and CD274 suggests a modulation of interferon signaling, pyroptosis, and T cell activity, potentially contributing to the inflammatory response. SMAD4’s influence on FN1 suggests a role in regulating cell adhesion, ECM remodeling, and tissue repair. ATF3 acts as an integrator, connecting these pathways. The complement system (C1QA/C1QB) plays a crucial role in initiating and amplifying the inflammatory response. The interactions between these components suggest a complex feedback loop that contributes to the sustained inflammation characteristic of uveitis.

2.3. Hypothesis

We hypothesize that the observed changes in gene expression in peripheral blood from uveitis patients reflect a systemic dysregulation of immune responses, complement activation, and cell adhesion processes. Specifically, we propose that dysregulation of RELB, SMAD4, and ATF3 activity leads to altered expression of their target genes (GBP5, CD274, FN1, and components of the complement system), resulting in an amplified inflammatory response, impaired immune regulation, altered ECM remodeling, and disrupted cell adhesion. This contributes to the development and progression of uveitis. The specific etiology of uveitis influences the specific pattern of DEG expression and the relative contribution of these pathways. Further investigation is needed to determine the precise causal relationships and the role of other potential upstream regulators and signaling pathways. The peripheral blood gene expression profile likely reflects systemic changes, but further studies are needed to confirm the specific involvement of these pathways in the ocular inflammation characteristic of uveitis.
